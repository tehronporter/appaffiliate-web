# Phase C: Messaging + Copy Strategy

## 1) Executive Summary

AppAffiliate needs to sound like a finished SaaS for affiliate operations, not a capable internal tool explaining its own structure. The product already covers real workflows: Apple ingestion readiness, partners, promo codes, unattributed review, attribution decisions, commissions, payouts, payout batches, exports, settings, audit, and a read-only partner portal. Phase C locks how AppAffiliate should talk about that reality before any page-level redesign begins.

The messaging system follows the Phase A direction: TEHSO minimalism, Notion-like restraint, and a finance-first calm bias. That means quieter language, sharper structure, and more trust-forward framing. AppAffiliate should feel clear, precise, and operational. It should not sound loud, vague, overly automated, or aspirational beyond its actual MVP scope.

This phase is still strategy work. It does not fully rewrite production pages yet. Its job is to give Phase D a decision-ready copy system for public pages, logged-in admin workflows, and the read-only partner portal.

**A) Core Positioning Summary**

- AppAffiliate is an affiliate operations platform for app teams that need cleaner attribution review, commission control, payout preparation, and partner visibility.
- It sits between partner activity and finance execution, turning scattered review work into one calmer operating system.
- It is built for teams that need trust, control, and readable workflow state more than marketing theater.
- It is not an influencer marketplace, a self-serve billing platform, or a fully automated adtech engine.

## 2) Core Positioning

### One-sentence positioning statement

AppAffiliate gives app teams a calmer way to run affiliate operations, from partner codes and attribution review to commissions, payouts, exports, and partner visibility.

### One-paragraph positioning statement

AppAffiliate is a finance-aware affiliate operations platform for app teams running partner programs that need more control than a generic dashboard provides. It brings Apple ingestion readiness, partner and code management, unattributed review, commission decisions, payout preparation, exports, and a read-only partner portal into one focused operating layer. The goal is not more noise or more automation claims. The goal is clearer workflow state, better review discipline, and a more trustworthy path from attribution to payout.

### Category framing

- Primary category: affiliate operations software for app teams
- Secondary framing: attribution review and payout preparation workspace
- Supporting framing: partner visibility layer for codes, performance, and payout status

### Target audience summary

- app founders and growth leads who need visibility across affiliate operations
- affiliate and program managers running partner relationships, codes, and review workflows
- finance and operations teams who need commissions, payout readiness, and export control
- partners who need a simple read-only view into their codes, performance, and payouts

### Key problem statement

Affiliate programs often break down between attribution, manual review, commission decisions, payout prep, and partner communication. Teams end up stitching together spreadsheets, inbox decisions, internal notes, and partial dashboards with too little trust in what is final. AppAffiliate gives those workflows a single operating system with clearer state, narrower review steps, and safer partner visibility.

### Key promise / value proposition

AppAffiliate helps teams run affiliate operations with more trust and less workflow drift. It makes review queues readable, commission state explicit, payout preparation cleaner, exports finance-ready, and partner visibility safer.

### What AppAffiliate is not

- not an influencer marketplace
- not a generic CRM
- not a self-serve Stripe billing suite
- not a broad adtech optimization engine
- not a tax, legal, or remittance platform
- not “just a dashboard”

## 5) Voice and Tone Rules

### Core voice

AppAffiliate should sound:

- calm
- precise
- direct
- restrained
- trustworthy
- premium
- human

AppAffiliate should not sound:

- loud
- breathless
- overly clever
- corporate
- startup-hyped
- technical for its own sake

### Tone by context

#### Homepage / headlines

- concise
- confident
- benefit-first
- category-clear

Use short declarative language. Do not try to win attention with hype.

#### Feature explanations

- structured
- specific
- low-drama
- proof-oriented

Explain what the workflow does and why it matters. Keep it concrete.

#### Empty states

- calm
- helpful
- lightly directional

State what is missing, why the screen is empty, and the next useful step. Avoid “nothing here yet” filler.

#### Action confirmations

- brief
- explicit
- reassuring

State what changed and what the new state means.

#### Error states

- clear
- respectful
- actionable

Describe the problem plainly and tell the user what to do next. Do not over-apologize or use vague failure language.

#### Onboarding / setup guidance

- operational
- confidence-building
- checklist-oriented

Frame setup as readiness work, not magical activation.

#### Finance review workflows

- precise
- sober
- low-emotion

Prioritize status clarity over warmth. The tone should remain human, but never casual.

#### Partner portal copy

- simple
- transparent
- reassuring

The portal should explain status without leaking internal process detail.

#### Trust / security copy

- quiet
- credible
- measured

Lead with what exists today. Avoid inflated compliance claims.

### Sentence-level rules

- Prefer shorter sentences.
- Use plain verbs.
- Use fewer adjectives.
- Name the workflow directly.
- Keep one main idea per sentence.
- Choose clarity over cleverness.

### Rhythm rules

- Headlines should feel compact and resolved.
- Subheadlines should explain the claim without sounding like a paragraph.
- Body copy should stay readable, with visible breathing room between ideas.
- Microcopy should sound deliberate, not rushed.

**D) Voice / Tone Rules**

- Calm over energetic
- Direct over promotional
- Trustworthy over trendy
- Specific over abstract
- Human over corporate
- Minimal over sparse

## 9) CTA Strategy

### Best CTA language for public site

- `Request access`
- `Book demo`
- `View product`
- `How it works`
- `View security`
- `Login`

### Best CTA language for internal app

- `Open workspace`
- `Review queue`
- `Create partner`
- `Create code`
- `Approve`
- `Reject`
- `Create payout batch`
- `Export`

### Best CTA language for partner portal

- `Open portal`
- `View codes`
- `View performance`
- `View payouts`

### Primary vs secondary CTA rules

- one primary CTA per section or action cluster
- primary CTA should represent the next best step, not the loudest button
- secondary CTA should provide exploration or confirmation, not compete with the primary action
- destructive or high-consequence actions should never share the same visual priority as the primary positive action

### When to use each CTA

- `Book demo`: use when the audience is evaluating the product and likely wants guided review
- `Request access`: use when the audience is qualified and ready to start a conversation or get invited
- `Login`: use only for existing users
- `Open workspace`: use after authentication for internal admin users
- `Open portal`: use after authentication for partner users

**H) CTA Strategy**

- Public CTAs should stay quiet and clear.
- Internal CTAs should reflect workflow actions, not marketing language.
- Portal CTAs should mostly help navigation, not suggest self-serve management.
- Use `Book demo` for guided evaluation and `Request access` for access intent.

## 10) Copy Do / Don’t Guide

### Headlines

Do:

- state the workflow clearly
- lead with clarity, control, or trust
- keep them short

Do not:

- stack buzzwords
- oversell automation
- sound like generic B2B growth software

### Buttons

Do:

- use short action verbs
- reflect the real next step

Do not:

- use vague labels like `Continue journey`
- hide consequence behind unclear language

### Nav labels

Do:

- use plain product nouns
- keep labels stable across pages

Do not:

- use internal project phrasing
- rename familiar workflow objects for style

### Settings copy

Do:

- say what is configurable, read-only, or not yet available
- keep explanations brief

Do not:

- imply settings save when they do not
- turn settings pages into product marketing

### Finance copy

Do:

- use exact status language
- make state transitions explicit

Do not:

- use casual or playful phrasing
- soften critical finance meaning with vague synonyms

### Portal copy

Do:

- center on the partner’s own records
- explain status simply

Do not:

- expose internal review framing
- mention admin-only controls or audit language

### Empty states

Do:

- explain why the screen is empty
- offer the next useful action when one exists

Do not:

- default to “nothing here yet”
- create fake optimism when the state is actually unconfigured

### Alerts and confirmations

Do:

- be explicit about what changed
- help the user recover or continue

Do not:

- overuse warning tone
- rely on generic “something went wrong” copy

**I) Copy Do / Don’t Guide**

- Use plain, product-true language.
- Keep status and workflow words stable.
- Remove internal project vocabulary from user-facing surfaces.
- Keep partner copy simple and finance copy exact.
